What Is a Safe Withdrawal Rate Calculator?

A safe withdrawal rate calculator determines the percentage of your retirement corpus you can withdraw annually without running out of money over your lifetime. The most famous rule is the 4% rule: withdraw 4% of your initial portfolio in the first year, then adjust for inflation each subsequent year. This rule was derived from historical US market data and is designed to last 30 years.

Our safe withdrawal rate retirement tool goes beyond the simple 4% rule. You can enter your own withdrawal rate, adjust for life expectancy, expected portfolio returns, inflation, and even incorporate flexible spending. The calculator also shows the success probability (if Monte Carlo simulation is included) or the maximum sustainable withdrawal rate for your specific situation.

How to Use This Safe Withdrawal Calculator

1 Enter your retirement corpus – the total savings you have at retirement.
2 Enter your desired annual withdrawal or desired withdrawal rate (as a percentage).
3 Set expected portfolio return – e.g., 5-7% for a balanced portfolio during retirement.
4 Set inflation rate – typically 2-3% for developed economies, 4-6% for emerging markets.
5 Enter the number of years in retirement – your life expectancy minus retirement age.
6 Click Calculate – the tool shows the yearly withdrawal amount, the ending balance after the period, and whether your withdrawal rate is sustainable.

Frequently Asked Questions About Safe Withdrawal Rates

1. What is a safe withdrawal rate (SWR)?
The safe withdrawal rate is the percentage of your initial retirement portfolio that you can withdraw annually (adjusted for inflation) without depleting the portfolio over a given retirement horizon.
2. What is the 4% rule?
The 4% rule says you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in the first year of retirement and then adjust that dollar amount for inflation each subsequent year, and the portfolio should last 30 years.
3. Is the 4% rule still valid today?
For US retirees, many experts now recommend 3-3.5% due to lower expected returns and longer lifespans. For other countries with higher inflation, a lower rate (3%) may be safer.
4. How does inflation affect the safe withdrawal rate?
Higher inflation erodes purchasing power faster, requiring a lower initial withdrawal rate to sustain the same real spending. Our calculator accounts for inflation in all projections.
5. What is the safe withdrawal rate for early retirement (50+ years)?
For retirements lasting 40-50 years, a sustainable withdrawal rate often drops to 3-3.5%. Some conservative FIRE enthusiasts use 2.5-3%.
6. Can I withdraw a higher percentage if I have a flexible spending plan?
Yes, “variable withdrawal” strategies can start with a higher rate (e.g., 5%) but reduce spending in down years. Our calculator models fixed inflation‑adjusted withdrawals, which is the classic SWR.
7. Does portfolio allocation affect the safe withdrawal rate?
Yes. A balanced portfolio (50-70% equity) has historically supported higher SWRs than all‑bond portfolios. Our calculator lets you input an expected return, which implicitly reflects your allocation.
8. What is the success probability of the 4% rule?
Based on historical US data, a 4% withdrawal rate had a >95% success rate for 30-year retirements. For 40 years, success drops to ~85%.
9. Can I use this calculator for any currency?
Yes – select your currency (INR, USD, EUR, GBP, PKR, etc.). The withdrawal amounts and corpus are displayed in that currency.
10. How do I calculate the safe withdrawal rate for a couple?
Enter the combined corpus and combined expenses. For longevity, consider the longer of the two life expectancies (e.g., plan for 30-35 years if both retire at 60).
11. Does the calculator assume you leave no inheritance?
The standard 4% rule aims to have a near‑zero balance at the end. You can adjust to a lower withdrawal rate if you want to preserve principal for heirs.
12. What is the maximum safe withdrawal rate for a 25‑year retirement?
For 25 years, a 4.5% withdrawal rate may be safe with a balanced portfolio. The shorter the retirement, the higher the sustainable withdrawal rate.
13. How does social security or pension affect the withdrawal rate?
You can reduce your required withdrawal amount by the annual pension/SS income. Then calculate the withdrawal rate on the remaining corpus needed.
14. What is a “flexible” withdrawal rate?
Instead of a fixed inflation‑adjusted amount, you withdraw a percentage of the portfolio each year (e.g., 4% of current balance). This never depletes but income varies with markets.
15. Does the calculator account for taxes?
No – it shows pre‑tax withdrawal. Reduce your expected return or increase required withdrawal to account for taxes.
16. Can I save or share my SWR calculation?
Yes – click “Share” to copy a URL with your inputs, or download a CSV of the withdrawal schedule.
17. What is the Trinity Study?
The Trinity Study (1998) popularised the 4% rule by analysing historical US stock and bond returns over 30‑year periods. It found that a 4% withdrawal rate had a high probability of success.
18. Is this calculator free?
Yes – 100% free, no signup, no ads. Use it for personal retirement planning or professional advice.
19. Can I use this for a trust or endowment?
Yes – same principle applies. For perpetual withdrawal (infinite horizon), use a rate equal to the real return of the portfolio (e.g., 3% real return → 3% withdrawal).
20. How accurate is the calculator?
It uses the same mathematical formulas as financial planning software. However, future returns are uncertain. Use it as a planning guide, not a guarantee.
