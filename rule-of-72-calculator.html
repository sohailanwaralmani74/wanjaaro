What Is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a simple financial approximation that estimates the number of years required to double an investment at a fixed annual rate of return. You divide 72 by the annual interest rate (as a whole number) to get the approximate doubling time. For example, at 8% return, 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years. This rule of 72 calculator does the math for you and also includes a reverse mode: given a target doubling period, find the required annual return.

The rule works best for rates between 6% and 10%, but our calculator provides accurate results for any positive rate using the exact compound interest formula (logarithms). Use it to quickly compare investments, estimate retirement growth, or plan financial goals.

Frequently Asked Questions About the Rule of 72

1. What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a mental math shortcut that estimates doubling time: divide 72 by the annual return percentage. For example, at 9% return, 72/9 = 8 years to double.
2. How accurate is the Rule of 72?
It is most accurate for returns between 6% and 10% (error < 0.1 year). For 2% or 20%, error is about 1 year. Our calculator uses the exact formula for precision.
3. What is the formula for exact doubling time?
Exact time = ln(2) / ln(1 + rate/100). For example, at 8%, ln(2)/ln(1.08) = 9.006 years. The Rule of 72 approximates this.
4. Can I use the Rule of 72 for inflation?
Yes – it shows how long it takes for prices to double at a given inflation rate. For 6% inflation, prices double in ~12 years (72/6).
5. What is the reverse Rule of 72?
Reverse mode: given a target doubling period, find the required annual return. For example, to double in 5 years, you need 72/5 = 14.4% return (exact: ~14.87%).
6. Why 72? Why not 70 or 69?
72 is convenient because it has many divisors (1,2,3,4,6,8,9,12…). For continuous compounding, the number would be 69.3, but 72 gives better accuracy for typical rates.
7. Does the Rule of 72 work for SIP (monthly contributions)?
No – the rule assumes a lump sum. For periodic investments, use a SIP calculator or XIRR. Our tool focuses on lump‑sum doubling.
8. What is a realistic doubling time for stock market investments?
At 10% historical equity return, money doubles every 7.2 years. At 12%, it doubles every 6 years. Using the exact formula, 10% gives 7.27 years, 12% gives 6.12 years.
9. Can the Rule of 72 be used for negative returns?
No – it only works for positive growth. For losses, you would compute “halving time” but that’s not commonly used.
10. How does compounding frequency affect doubling time?
Our calculator assumes annual compounding. For monthly compounding, the effective annual rate is slightly higher, so doubling time is marginally shorter. But the Rule of 72 remains a good approximation.
11. What is the difference between Rule of 72 and Rule of 70?
Rule of 70 uses 70 as numerator – more accurate for lower rates (2‑4%). Rule of 72 is better for 6‑10%, and Rule of 73 is sometimes used for 15‑20%.
12. Can I use this calculator to find tripling time?
The Rule of 72 only doubles. For tripling, use the rule of 114 (114/rate). Our exact calculator can compute any multiple – enter 3 as the multiplication factor.
13. Is the Rule of 72 taught in finance courses?
Yes – it’s a classic shortcut in personal finance, investment analysis, and retirement planning. It’s used for quick mental estimates before doing precise calculations.
14. How does the Rule of 72 apply to credit card debt?
If you carry a credit card balance at 24% interest, your debt doubles in 72/24 = 3 years. This illustrates the danger of high‑interest debt.
15. Can I use the calculator for any currency?
Yes – the rule works regardless of currency because it deals with percentages and time. Our calculator lets you choose your currency for output formatting.
16. What is the doubling time at 1% interest?
Exact: ln(2)/ln(1.01) ≈ 69.66 years. Rule of 72 would give 72 years – still a reasonable approximation for such a low rate.
17. Does the Rule of 72 work for fractional years?
The rule gives a decimal number, which you can interpret as years (e.g., 7.2 years). Our calculator also handles fractional rates and periods.
18. How do I interpret the chart?
The chart shows the growth of ₹1 (or any unit) over time. The point where the curve crosses 2 indicates the doubling time. You can see how the curve steepens with higher returns.
