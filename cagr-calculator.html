What Is a CAGR Calculator?

A CAGR calculator (Compound Annual Growth Rate calculator) measures the mean annual growth rate of an investment over a specified period longer than one year. It represents the rate at which your investment would have grown if it had grown at a steady rate each year – smoothing out volatility. The compound annual growth rate is widely used to compare historical returns of different assets (stocks, mutual funds, real estate, etc.) and to project future growth.

Unlike simple average returns, CAGR accounts for compounding, giving you a truthful picture of performance. Our compound growth rate calculator also includes a reverse CAGR function: given an end value and rate, find the required starting amount or years.

Frequently Asked Questions About CAGR Calculators

1. What is CAGR?
CAGR stands for Compound Annual Growth Rate. It is the geometric average annual return that smooths out an investment’s performance over a period, assuming steady growth each year.
2. How is CAGR calculated?
The CAGR formula is: CAGR = (End Value / Start Value)^(1 / Number of Years) – 1. The result is expressed as a percentage.
3. What is the difference between CAGR and average annual return?
Average annual return is the arithmetic mean of yearly returns, ignoring compounding. CAGR accounts for compounding and is always lower than or equal to the arithmetic average when returns are volatile.
4. Can I use CAGR for investments with periodic contributions (SIP)?
CAGR is designed for lump‑sum investments. For SIPs, use XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return). However, our sip cagr calculator (separate tool) can estimate CAGR‑like returns for monthly investments.
5. What is a good CAGR for equity mutual funds?
Historically, Indian equity funds have delivered 12‑15% CAGR over 10‑15 years. Large‑cap funds may give 10‑12%, while mid/small caps can be higher with more risk.
6. How do I calculate CAGR in Excel?
Use the formula =(End Value/Start Value)^(1/Years)-1 or the RRI function: =RRI(Periods, Start Value, End Value). Our online tool does the same without Excel.
7. What is reverse CAGR?
Reverse CAGR allows you to compute the starting value, ending value, or period length when the other three variables are known. For instance, "How much do I need to invest today to get ₹10L in 5 years at 12% CAGR?"
8. Can CAGR be negative?
Yes. If the ending value is less than the starting value, CAGR is negative – meaning the investment lost value on an annualised basis.
9. Is CAGR the same as annualised return?
Yes. Annualised return and CAGR are often used interchangeably. Both represent the geometric average annual growth rate.
10. How does inflation affect CAGR?
CAGR as calculated is nominal. To get real CAGR, adjust the ending value for inflation: Real CAGR = (End Value / (1+inflation)^years / Start Value)^(1/years) – 1.
11. What is the difference between CAGR and XIRR?
CAGR assumes a single lump sum at start. XIRR handles multiple cash flows (like SIPs) at irregular intervals. Use CAGR for lump sums, XIRR for periodic investments.
12. Can I calculate CAGR for stocks using this calculator?
Yes. Enter the stock’s purchase price as start value, current price as end value, and holding period in years. This gives the annualised return from price appreciation (excluding dividends).
13. How accurate is CAGR for short periods?
CAGR is mathematically precise for any period, but for less than one year you should annualise with caution. Our calculator works for fractions of years (e.g., 0.5 years).
14. What is a 5‑year CAGR calculator?
A 5 year cagr calculator simply sets the number of years to 5. It shows the annualised return over that specific period – useful for comparing medium‑term fund performance.
15. Can I use CAGR for a lump sum with reinvested dividends?
Yes – include reinvested dividends in the ending value. The CAGR will reflect total return (price + dividends) as a single growth rate.
16. Why does CAGR sometimes look lower than the simple average?
Because CAGR accounts for compounding and volatility drag. In volatile series, the arithmetic mean overstates true growth, while CAGR gives a realistic, lower figure.
17. Does the calculator support decimal years (e.g., 1.5 years)?
Yes – you can enter fractional years (e.g., 1.5 for 18 months). The CAGR formula works with any positive real number.
18. What is the difference between CAGR and annualised return in mutual funds?
In mutual funds, “annualised return” is often the CAGR. Fund houses typically report past 1‑year, 3‑year, 5‑year, and 10‑year returns as CAGR.
