Why Understanding Interest Matters

Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the reward for saving and investing. Whether you're earning interest on a savings account, paying interest on a loan, or investing for the future, understanding how simple interest and compound interest work is essential for making smart financial decisions.

This interest calculator shows you exactly how much interest you'll earn or pay — whether you choose simple interest (linear growth) or compound interest (exponential growth). All calculations are in your local currency with support for 170+ currencies.

The Interest Formulas

Understanding the interest formula helps you appreciate how your money grows:

Simple Interest Formula

I = P × r × t Where: I = Interest, P = Principal, r = Rate (as decimal), t = Time in years

For example, if you invest $10,000 at 8% simple interest for 5 years, you'll earn $4,000 in interest — a linear growth of $800 each year.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P × (1 + r/n)n×t Where: A = Final amount, P = Principal, r = Rate (as decimal), n = Compounding frequency, t = Time in years

For example, if you invest $10,000 at 8% compounded monthly for 5 years, you'll earn $4,900 in interest — $900 more than simple interest.

Frequently Asked Questions About Interest

1. What is the difference between simple and compound interest?
Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount — growth is linear. Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus accumulated interest — growth is exponential. This simple interest calculator lets you compare both.
2. How do I calculate simple interest?
Use the simple interest formula: I = P × r × t. For example, $10,000 at 8% for 5 years = $4,000 interest. Or simply use this interest calculator — it does the math instantly.
3. Why does compound interest grow faster than simple interest?
Compound interest grows faster because you earn interest on interest. Each period, the interest earned is added to the principal, so the next period's interest is calculated on a larger amount. Over time, this creates exponential growth.
4. What compounding frequency is best?
Higher compounding frequencies yield more interest. Daily compounding yields the highest returns, followed by weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annually. This interest rate calculator lets you compare all frequencies.
5. Can I use this calculator for loan interest?
Yes. This loan interest calculator works for both simple and compound interest loans. Just enter the loan amount as the principal, the interest rate, and the loan term to see how much interest you'll pay.
6. What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a quick way to estimate how long it takes for money to double at a given interest rate. Divide 72 by the annual interest rate. For example, at 8%, money doubles in about 9 years. This compound interest calculator shows the exact doubling time.
7. How accurate is this interest calculator?
This interest amount calculator uses standard financial formulas and is accurate for planning. However, actual interest rates and compounding methods may vary — always check with your bank or lender for final numbers.
