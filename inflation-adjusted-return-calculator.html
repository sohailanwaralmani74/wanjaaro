What Is an Inflation Adjusted Return Calculator?

An inflation adjusted return calculator shows the true growth of your investment after removing the eroding effect of inflation. While your nominal return might look impressive (e.g., 12% from stocks), inflation can eat away a large portion of that gain. The real rate of return tells you how much your purchasing power actually increased. This tool uses the Fisher equation: Real Return = (1 + Nominal Return) / (1 + Inflation Rate) – 1.

Whether you are evaluating a stock market investment, a fixed deposit, or a bond, understanding the inflation adjusted stock market return is critical for long‑term planning. Our calculator also shows a year‑by‑year comparison of nominal vs real balances, helping you visualise the impact of inflation over time.

Frequently Asked Questions About Inflation Adjusted Returns

1. What is an inflation adjusted return?
An inflation adjusted return (real return) is the growth of your investment after accounting for the loss of purchasing power due to inflation. It shows how much more you can actually buy compared to the start.
2. How do you calculate inflation adjusted return?
The formula is: Real Return = (1 + Nominal Return) / (1 + Inflation Rate) – 1. For example, 12% nominal return with 6% inflation gives a real return of (1.12/1.06) – 1 = 5.66%.
3. What is a typical inflation adjusted stock market return?
Historically, the S&P 500 has returned about 10% nominal and 7% real (after 3% inflation). For Indian equities (Nifty 50), long‑term real returns have been around 6–8% after adjusting for 5‑6% inflation.
4. Can real return be negative?
Yes. If inflation is higher than your nominal return (e.g., 3% return but 5% inflation), the real return is negative – you lose purchasing power even though your balance grew nominally.
5. How does inflation affect long‑term investments?
Inflation compounds over time, eroding purchasing power exponentially. A 6% inflation rate halves the real value of money every 12 years. This is why you need investments that beat inflation.
6. What is the Fisher equation?
The Fisher equation describes the relationship between nominal interest rates, real interest rates, and inflation. The exact form used here is: (1 + nominal) = (1 + real) × (1 + inflation).
7. Why is nominal return misleading without inflation adjustment?
Because the same number of rupees buys less in the future. A 12% nominal return with 10% inflation leaves you only 2% real gain – a much smaller improvement in actual wealth.
8. Does this calculator work for any currency?
Yes – select your currency (USD, INR, EUR, GBP, etc.). Inflation adjustment is independent of currency; the real return formula remains the same.
9. What inflation rate should I use for long‑term planning?
Use a conservative estimate based on your country's historical average: 6% for India, 3% for US/EU, 4–5% for many emerging markets. You can also run multiple scenarios with different rates.
10. Can I calculate inflation adjusted return for a portfolio with multiple assets?
This calculator assumes a single lump sum with a constant nominal return. For a portfolio, first calculate your weighted average nominal return, then apply inflation adjustment.
11. Does the calculator account for taxes?
No – it shows pre‑tax returns. For post‑tax real returns, first reduce your nominal return by the applicable tax rate (e.g., 10% LTCG for equity), then apply inflation adjustment.
12. What is the difference between real return and nominal return?
Nominal return is the percentage increase in your rupee/dollar balance. Real return is the percentage increase in your purchasing power – what matters most for retirement and long‑term goals.
13. How accurate is the Fisher equation for small inflation rates?
For typical inflation rates (0–15%), the Fisher equation is highly accurate. For hyperinflation, the exact geometric formula is still correct – and our calculator uses it.
14. Can I use this for debt funds or fixed deposits?
Absolutely. Enter the FD or bond’s nominal rate (e.g., 7%) and your expected inflation (e.g., 6%). The real return may be very small or negative after taxes.
15. How do I interpret the chart?
The chart shows two lines: nominal balance (grows faster) and real balance (grows slower). The gap between them widens over time, visually demonstrating the erosion caused by inflation.

17. Does the calculator support negative inflation (deflation)?
Yes – you can enter negative inflation rates (e.g., -1%). In deflation, real returns can be higher than nominal returns.
18. What is the maximum investment period allowed?
Up to 50 years – sufficient for most retirement and generational wealth planning.
